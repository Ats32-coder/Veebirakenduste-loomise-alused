Yakitori

Yakitori (literally "grilled bird") is a classic Japanese dish of bite-sized chicken pieces skewered on bamboo sticks and grilled over a charcoal fire. It is a beloved staple of Japanese street food, festivals, and Izakaya (informal pubs).



Key Characteristics

  • Ingredients: While "yakitori" technically refers to chicken, it encompasses nearly every part of the bird—from juicy thigh meat (momo) and wings (tebasaki) to crispy skin (kawa) and minced meatballs (tsukune).
  • The Grill: For the most authentic flavor, it is cooked over Binchōtan, a high-quality white charcoal that burns at an extremely high heat without smoke, sealing in juices and adding a distinct aroma.
    • Seasoning Options: Diners typically choose between two styles: Shio (Salt): Simple sea salt that highlights the natural flavor of the meat. Tare (Sauce): A sweet and savory glaze made from soy sauce, mirin, sake, and sugar.

Popular Varieties

  • Negima: The most iconic variety, featuring alternating pieces of chicken thigh and leek (negi).
  • Tsukune: Succulent chicken meatballs often seasoned with ginger or shiso and served with a raw egg yolk for dipping.
  • Kawa: Strips of fatty chicken skin grilled until golden and crunchy.
  • Reba: Tender chicken liver, which is especially rich when paired with tare sauce.
